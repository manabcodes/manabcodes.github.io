Rethinking China's Approach to Privacy

DCU Law and Tech Research Cluster Podcast, Season 1, Episode 1
Host: Felipe Alvarez Vega

China Digital Rights Privacy Law EU-China Relations AI Regulation

About this episode

China is the country that at the same time created Alibaba, TikTok, and the world's largest video gaming industry, and that mandates all its companies and citizens to cooperate with intelligence work. Western coverage collapses this complexity into surveillance panic. The documents tell a more complicated story.

In this pilot episode of the DCU Law and Tech podcast, I argued that the West's default posture of antagonism toward China, treating it as simply a surveillance state in contrast to a rights-based EU or a supposed land of freedom on the other side of the Atlantic, misses what Chinese government documents actually say about rights, collective identity, and their own conception of digital governance. That doesn't mean taking them at face value. It means engaging with what they actually claim. Jiāolóng dé yúnyǔ, zhōng fēi chí zhōng wù yě ...

Selected Quotes

On why China, not Bangladesh
"China is like 200 kilometres away from Bangladesh. Yet culturally and strategically, China sometimes feels very far away — because China operates completely differently from, for example, my neighbour India. India is like a working democracy, a democracy in progress. But China has achieved remarkable economic success in the last 30 years while carrying actual proven claims of human rights violations on its plate. It's a country that at the same time inspires awe and is a very enigmatic figure."
On Chinese government documents
"If you actually read Chinese documents from the Chinese government — which is very hard to find in English translations, but with Google Translate you can basically upload them and pray to God the translation comes out fairly good enough — what you find is that they claim to be not a state-centric regime but a rights-based regime. Just like the EU. But with a different conception of rights."
On socialist values in AI regulation
"China has interim remarks on how to train generative AI, and they said the data used cannot violate socialist values. When I first read it I was like — socialist values? Everyone cites Reuters on this, and Reuters said: this is bad, this just wants to surveil people. But when you look at the document, the surveillance aspect is very small. Most of it is about socialist values. And within the footnotes, as an explanation, they say: not liking pornography is a socialist value. You cannot train AI on pornography because it violates socialist norms. And I was like — in what world is anti-pornography a socialist behaviour?"
On Chinese family-centrism vs state-centrism
"China is not just a state-centric country. Their documents keep saying: talk to your family, you have to give your parents' consent to process your data. They have a huge emphasis on society working as a whole. For example — and this is not related to digital law, but I have a lot of LGBT friends — the Chinese transgender law says that if you want to transition, you have to get approval from your family members. So Chinese policies are not just state-centric. They are very family-centric."
On structural similarity between EU and China
"When you forget about the values — if you see it completely from the outside — the approaches are very similar. Both the EU and China are trying to protect their market, regulate data flows, and impose standards on platforms. It's just that the definition of rights for EU and China is very different."
On what Europe should do
"Since China is here to stay, Europe should be more cautious than simply joining a world polarised between China and the US. If it really feels it's a mediator, then the mediation should be less one-sided. I'm not saying let's hug. I'm saying it should be a cooperation. Sceptical cooperation — especially when it comes to human rights. But Chinese companies should not be treated any more differently than US companies."
A note on the trans rights detour: In this episode I mentioned, almost in passing, that Chinese transgender law requires family consent for gender-affirming surgery — even for adults. I brought it up as an example of China's family-centrism rather than pure state-centrism. Subsequent reporting confirms this is accurate, and that the requirement persists in practice even after 2022 regulatory reforms. I raised this purely as an abstract academic observation, in the same detached and impersonal way that one might note, say, that Mexico City is in Mexico and Kansas City is in Kansas.
